From Infinity to Simplicity

In this informal essay, I briefly examine materials related to the concepts of infinity, recursion, complexity, and simplicity.

Infinity in mathematics is a concept that comes in at some point in our education probably as “…”. We are comfortable with the notion of “real number line” \(\mathbb{R}\) or the “set of all natural numbers” \(\mathbb{N}\). However, we rarely revisit and use the axioms that constitute the foundations of computer science, calculus, etc., that is zfc set theory. The gap between the real world (our physical measurements and observations) and the idea of infinity is getting larger. We know that the speed of light is not infinite, and also physicists suspect that the whole universe is finite.[5] There is also no consensus on the infinitesimal either, as some scientists propose that space-time is discrete.[6] Even if there is no infinity in Physics, it does not follow that the abstract idea of infinity is false. But along with the complications of the Continuum Hypothesis, one should at least acknowledge that possibility.

Here is the definition of a set by Cantor[14]:

A set is a gathering together into a whole of definite, distinct objects of our perception [Anschauung] or of our thought—which are called elements of the set. (Wikipedia tranlation)


This definition is too encompassing and leads to contradictions such as Berry's paradox, or Russell's paradox. The further elaborately developed zfc set theory offers a better ground. But, for example, the axiom of choice is also controversial as it gives rise to the Banach –Tarski paradox.

Studying infinities goes back to the ancient Greeks. Aristotle defines two types of infinities: potential infinity and actual infinity. (the two following definitions are taken from [17]) Potential infinity refers to a procedure that gets closer and closer to, but never quite reaches, an infinite end. For instance, the sequence of numbers $$1, 2, 3, … $$ Actual infinity, is an infinity that one actually reaches; the process is already done. For instance, let's put braces around that sequence mentioned earlier: $$ \{1, 2, 3, ...\} $$ Aristotle did not believe in the existence of any actual infinity, and hence would not like the new notion of Cantor’s sets. Some mathematicians like Leopold Kronecker, never accepted the controversial ideas of Cantor. For a further discussion on the existence of infinity refer to [18, 19].

But let us take set theory for granted and enter the Cantor’s Paradise! One of the interesting results is that the size of natural numbers set is equal to the size of even numbers (they have the same cardinality), since there exists a one-to-one correspondence between the two sets. (Frege names this idea, Hume’s Principle) Another result is that \(\mathbb{R}\) and \(\mathbb{N}\) have different cardinalities, as there can be no bijection between them, according to Cantor's famous diagonal argument. There are other aspects of recursion and infinity inside languages and cultures. Lasnik argues that: “Infinity is one of the most fundamental properties of human languages, maybe the most fundamental one. People debate what the true universals of language are, but indisputably, infinity is central.” There is no bound or limit on a sentence’s length, as one can always embed a structure within another. Or as Chomsky asserts: “Language is, at its core, a system that is both digital and infinite."

In fact infinity arises in many various ways and has been studied by different philosophers as well. Plato, for example realized that in order to understand the definition of some word, one has to understand the definitions of the words inside. So we either end up in a circular loop, or an infinite regress. The way out of this infinite regress, Plato believes, is to accept that there are some abstract concepts, or platonic forms, that are not understood by their definitions, but through the process of recollection. This kind of knowledge is in fact remembering something that is already within us. He says that these ideas come from our previous existence, and we only recollect what we have forgotten, by encountering imperfect realizations of them in this world. It is interesting to note the ironical way that Simmias asks for the explanation of a platonic form. "But, Cebes" said Simmias, "what were the proofs of this? Remind me, for I do not recollect very well just now.”[15] And that in some sense implies that a platonic form is a platonic form! Another recursion here!
In math and axiomatic systems, when we want to prove a theorem, we regress backwards, and the knowledge that stops us from recursing forever, is the set of axioms that are accepted to be self-evidently true. The set of axioms define a theory.

“I think, therefore I am”, is another famous self-referential argument by René Descartes that leads to an infinite regress, and that is the reason of objection among many philosophers such as Hobbes.

“The question may be put infinitely, how do you know that you know, that you know, that you know? &c,” argued Hobbes. “Wherefore . . .we cannot separate thought from thinking matter, it seems rather to follow, that a thinking thing is material, than that ’tis immaterial.”[16]

Another argument that invokes an infinite regress can be the following. Let us imagine that we have a vicious serial killer. Is it her fault that she has made the bad decisions? If no, then she has no responsibility. If yes, is it her fault that she is a person that wills to make those bad decisions? If no, then she has no responsibility. If yes, is it her fault that … And you can see the only way to get out of this infinite regress, is to admit there is no free will.

There are many patterns in nature that seem to be limitlessly complex. Inside the human body itself, each of the respiratory system, dendritic trees of the nervous system, and circulatory system are immensely complex. Just a single Purkinje cell can have up to 200 000 connections to granule cells. [11]

As another example, all cells within the body are at most five cells away from a blood vessel. The circulatory systems is almost everywhere but it only makes up less than 5% of the body mass.[25] These astonishing optimal solutions are only possible in the realm of fractals and fractional dimensions. But how does this enormous complexity fit inside our limited genetic code? The answer is that fractals have extremely simple scale-free local rules (instructions, or genes) from which complexity emerges recursively. The very same idea has helped computer graphic designers to program intricate and realistic images of landscapes, and objects, using a few lines of code.

The advancement of science has made the explanation of many complex phenomena simpler and simpler. In the following paragraph from Discourse on Metaphysics, Leibniz also explains the same concept in different words and asserts that we should seek simple and short descriptions.

"The simplicity of God’s ways relates to the means he adopts, while their variety, richness or abundance relate to ends or effects. These should be in balance with one another, as the money for putting up a building has to be balanced against its desired size and beauty. Admittedly, whatever God does costs him nothing, even less than it costs a philosopher [here = ‘scientist’] to invent theories out of which to build his imaginary world, for God can bring a real world into existence merely by decreeing it. But in the exercise of wisdom, by God or a philosopher, there is something analogous to the cost of a building, namely the number of independent decrees or theories that are involved. For God’s creative activity to be economical is for it to involve very few separate decrees; for a scientific theory to be economical in its means is for it to have very few basic principles or axioms· Reason requires that multiplicity of hypotheses or principles be avoided, rather as the simplest system is always preferred in astronomy."

This concept, also known as Occam's razor, has been formalized by Solomonoff in a field called algorithmic probability that assigns higher prior probabilities to shorter descriptions of the data (a string). Also the measure of complexity of a string, Kolmogorov complexity, is the length of the shortest description that outputs the string. An infinitely long string might reduce to the simplest non-empty description, as “0.999…” is “exactly” equal to “1”, but a shorter version like 0.999 is harder to describe, and we have to use recursion (might be a for loop) Here is an example for Occum's razor: "Sun will rise tomorrow", is a better theory compared to: "Sun will rise tomorrow, if tomorrow is not 14/3/2022". As both of them work just fine till that day, but the latter is longer, and there is no extra evidence to believe that longer description. It really might be the case that there is something wrong with "14/3/2022" and the sun does not rise on that day, but before then, the first description must have a higher prior probability. Just in the same way, we should anticipate that the next bit of the string: "11111", is "1" as it will make the underlying code shorter.

One other way to express Leibniz's "simplicity of God's way", could be that “God” has done some kind of optimization over the Kolmogorov complexities and chosen the simplest rules! What restricts us from the romantic quest of this holy grail of explanation is that Kolmogorov complexity is uncomputable, as the existence of any program of finite length for computing it causes a paradox similar to Berry’s paradox!
